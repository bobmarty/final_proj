% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Measuring national sustainability through Environmental, Social, and Governance
(ESG) indicators is no longer an optional exercise. Rising regulatory pressure,
investor demand for transparency, and the expanding reach of climate
commitments have made ESG assessment a central component of how countries are
evaluated by international financial institutions, development banks, and
sovereign credit rating agencies. Despite this growing importance, most scoring
frameworks still depend on manual aggregation rules applied to heterogeneous
indicator sets, a practice that introduces inconsistency and fails to capture
the complex, nonlinear relationships embedded in cross-national sustainability
data.

This paper builds a prediction framework using a dataset drawn from three
internationally recognised sources --- UN Sustainable Development Goal (SDG)
indicators, World Bank Development Indicators, and aggregated ESG ratings ---
covering 193 countries between 2015 and 2020, yielding 5,897 country-year
observations. Five tabular machine learning models were trained and evaluated:
LightGBM, TabNet, TabPFN, TabM, and TabNSA. None of the individual models was
dominant across all evaluation conditions. A stacking ensemble combining
LightGBM and TabNet was then constructed, with combination weights determined
by constrained least-squares optimisation. LightGBM contributed approximately
sixty percent and TabNet approximately forty percent to the final prediction,
reflecting their complementary but unequal predictive contributions.

The hybrid model achieved a root mean squared error of 4.78, a mean absolute
error of 3.45, and a coefficient of determination of 0.92 on the held-out test
set, representing roughly eight to ten percent lower prediction error than the
best individual model. An Isolation Forest detector operating on the same
standardised feature set identified approximately five percent of observations
as sustainability risk anomalies. Feature attribution analysis showed that
governance indicators --- particularly government effectiveness and regulatory
quality --- account for nearly sixty percent of total predictive importance,
far outweighing the contribution of environmental and social variables.


\textbf{Methodological Upgrade:} To rectify the pervasive issue of target leakage in standard ESG interpolation, this study expands the dataset to include externally decoupled proxy targets and evaluates performance via a rigorous Rolling Window Cross-Validation protocol. Furthermore, the hybrid architecture is upgraded from a static weighted average into a Stacked Generalization Meta-Learner, integrating a Temporal Attention Network alongside LightGBM and TabNet to capture longitudinal macroeconomic momentum. Finally, the framework introduces an ESG Shock Detection module validated against the Sri Lankan sovereign default and the 2020 Global Pandemic, alongside a Causal Policy Simulator to quantify the exact trajectory lift of decarbonization interventions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} rise of responsible investment frameworks, ESG-linked
sovereign bonds, and climate-aligned development finance has turned national
ESG performance into a measurable outcome with real economic consequences. A
country's standing on major ESG indices now affects the terms at which it can
access international capital markets, the conditions attached to multilateral
lending, and its attractiveness to foreign direct investment from
ESG-screened institutional portfolios.

Translating this demand into reliable scores is genuinely difficult. National
ESG data arrives from a fragmented ecosystem of reporting agencies, UN
statistical offices, national statistical bureaus, and satellite monitoring
programmes. Coverage is uneven, reporting lags are common, and the same
underlying phenomenon --- say, the quality of environmental regulation --- is
proxied differently by different agencies. The relationships between raw
indicators and aggregate ESG scores are nonlinear, context-dependent, and not
stable across income groups or geographic regions. Conventional approaches,
including weighted linear composites and principal component analysis, impose
smoothness assumptions that the data frequently violates. They also require
continuous manual recalibration as the composition of reporting changes
year to year.

Machine learning has changed the practical calculus here. Gradient boosting
methods --- XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost --- have demonstrated strong
predictive accuracy on structured tabular datasets across financial modeling,
environmental risk scoring, and policy outcome prediction. They handle
heterogeneous feature scales naturally and are relatively robust to missing
data. More recently, a generation of neural architectures designed specifically
for tabular data has started to close the performance gap with tree-based
methods, particularly on datasets where complex feature interactions matter
more than simple monotonic relationships. The practical question is not which
type of model is better in the abstract, but whether combining both captures
something that neither can capture alone.

That question motivates this study. Rather than selecting a single model class,
this paper builds a hybrid ensemble that integrates LightGBM and TabNet through
a data-driven stacking procedure, uses Isolation Forest to layer a risk
detection capability on top of the regression output, and applies SHAP-based
attribution to identify which indicators actually drive predicted scores. The
specific contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A systematic benchmark comparison of five leading tabular learning
        models on a multi-source national ESG dataset covering 193 countries
        and six years.
  \item A hybrid stacking ensemble combining LightGBM and TabNet through
        constrained least-squares weight estimation, achieving lower prediction
        error than all five individual baselines.
  \item An unsupervised anomaly detection component using Isolation Forest to
        flag countries with structurally unusual ESG profiles as sustainability
        risk candidates, without requiring labelled anomaly examples.
  \item A dual feature attribution analysis using both LightGBM gain scores and
        SHAP values, revealing governance quality as the dominant driver of
        national ESG performance.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organised as follows. Section~II reviews related literature on
ESG modeling, tabular machine learning, and ensemble methods. Section~III
describes the dataset, preprocessing pipeline, model configurations, and
hybrid construction procedure. Section~IV presents all experimental results
including performance comparisons, ablation analysis, feature attribution, and
anomaly detection findings. Section~V concludes with practical implications
and directions for future research.

\section{Literature Review}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{ESG as a Measurable Construct}

The academic foundations of ESG measurement stretch back at least to Eccles,
Ioannou, and Serafeim \cite{eccles2014}, whose longitudinal study of publicly
listed firms established that organisations with strong sustainability practices
show superior financial performance and internal process quality over long
horizons. Their work gave empirical grounding to what had previously been
argued on principally normative grounds. At the national scale, Esty et al.\
\cite{esty2005} developed the Environmental Performance Index as one of the
first rigorous attempts to compare countries on measurable environmental
outcomes rather than stated commitments. Their findings highlighted governance
quality as a more consistent driver of environmental performance than income
level alone, a result that anticipates the feature importance findings of this
paper. Krosinsky \cite{krosinsky2012} carried the argument into portfolio
management, contending that ESG integration is a structural requirement for
sustainable long-term returns.

A persistent challenge in the field is the inconsistency of ESG ratings across
agencies. Berg, Koelbel, and Rigobon \cite{berg2022} quantified this problem
systematically, showing that cross-agency ESG rating correlations average
around 0.54 --- compared to correlations above 0.99 for credit ratings. They
attributed the divergence to differences in scope, measurement choices, and
aggregation methodology rather than to genuine disagreement about underlying
conditions. Kotsantonis and Serafeim \cite{kotsantonis2019} noted that ESG data
quality problems run deeper than agency disagreement: the same reported metric
frequently has different values across sources because measurement standards
are still fragmented. Escrig-Olmedo et al.\ \cite{escrig2019} evaluated the
methodologies of major rating agencies directly and found that social and
governance dimensions are captured in substantially different ways, adding
further noise to cross-agency comparisons. These findings collectively underpin
the motivation for building data-driven scoring models that are transparent,
reproducible, and not dependent on any single agency's judgements. Rau and
Yu \cite{rau2024} confirm that both institutional and retail demand for
methodologically rigorous ESG scoring has outpaced the available supply of
validated models.

\subsection{Machine Learning for ESG Prediction}

The application of machine learning to ESG prediction has developed rapidly
since the mid-2010s. Ferreira \cite{ferreira2017} documented the practical
toolkit --- regularised regression, ensemble methods, and cross-validation
workflows --- that now underpins most quantitative ESG research. Chen and
Guestrin \cite{chen2016} published XGBoost, whose computational efficiency and
strong performance on heterogeneous tabular data quickly made gradient boosting
the default modeling paradigm for financial prediction tasks.

D'Amato et al.\ \cite{damato2021} used Random Forest to predict corporate ESG
scores and noted that model-derived scores were more internally consistent than
human-assigned agency scores, suggesting that supervised machine learning can
reduce some of the subjectivity inherent in manual rating processes. Abdelfattah
et al.\ \cite{abdelfattah2025} demonstrated that incorporating country-level
macro variables alongside firm-level financial data meaningfully improved
prediction accuracy, directly motivating the multi-source data fusion strategy
used in this paper. Terzio\u{g}lu et al.\ \cite{terzioglu2025} applied SHAP
values to provide interpretable explanations for machine learning ESG
classifications, addressing the growing regulatory expectation that
automated scoring tools must be auditable. Liu et al.\ \cite{liu2023}
proposed combining gradient boosting with dimensionality reduction
for national-level ESG scoring, achieving competitive results across income
groups. Sultana et al.\ \cite{sultana2025} extended the approach to
unstructured data, showing that news-based ESG sentiment signals can
complement quantitative indicators when constructing composite scores.
Friede, Busch, and Bassen \cite{friede2015} conducted a meta-analysis of over
2,000 empirical studies and found a robust positive aggregate relationship
between ESG performance and financial outcomes, reinforcing the practical
importance of accurate ESG scoring tools.

\subsection{Tabular Deep Learning Architectures}

The design of neural networks for tabular data has become a distinct research
area. Standard multi-layer perceptrons treat all features uniformly, which
performs poorly when features are heterogeneous in type, scale, and
informativeness. TabNet, proposed by Arik and Pfister \cite{tabnet2021},
addresses this by using a sequential attention mechanism that selects a
different subset of features for each instance at each processing step. The
attention masks produced at each step can be read as per-instance feature
importance scores, which makes TabNet naturally interpretable --- an important
property in ESG applications where stakeholders need to understand what is
driving country-level scores.

TabPFN \cite{tabpfn2023} takes an entirely different approach: it is a
transformer pre-trained on approximately 18 million synthetic tabular datasets
generated from a Bayesian prior over tabular data-generating processes. At test
time, the model receives the training set as context and produces predictions
through a single forward pass, effectively performing Bayesian posterior
inference. This removes the need for any task-specific training and makes
TabPFN nearly hyperparameter-free, which is attractive for rapid deployment but
limits performance on larger datasets where the fixed pre-trained prior becomes
a bottleneck. TabM \cite{gorishniy2021} maintains multiple lightweight
parameter sets within a single model, achieving internal ensemble diversity at
low computational cost through its mini-batch ensemble design. TabNSA extends
the attention paradigm with a hard top-$k$ sparsity constraint that forces the
model to attend to at most $k$ features per step, reducing noise sensitivity
and producing naturally sparse attribution maps.

\subsection{Hybrid and Ensemble Learning}

The theoretical case for combining models with different inductive biases
rests on the well-established bias-variance decomposition: models that make
different errors can be combined to reduce total prediction error beyond
what any single model achieves. Stacking extends simple averaging by learning
how to combine base model outputs, rather than fixing the combination rule in
advance. Wang et al.\ \cite{wang2022} demonstrated the practical value of
this approach in credit risk modeling, where combining gradient boosting with
neural components produced substantially better performance than either
individually. Betti et al.\ \cite{betti2018} and Alsayegh et al.\
\cite{alsayegh2020} both found that ensemble approaches to ESG modeling
produce more stable and generalisable results across different sectors and
governance structures.

Despite this evidence, very few published studies have built hybrid ensembles
specifically for national-level ESG prediction that integrate modern tabular
deep learning architectures alongside gradient boosting. Most existing work
either uses a single model class or limits ensembling to combining variants
of the same method. This study directly fills that gap.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection in Environmental and Sustainability Data}

The identification of structurally anomalous observations in ESG datasets is
a separate but related challenge from score prediction. A country can receive
a well-predicted low score without being anomalous, and a high-scoring country
can exhibit sudden indicator deterioration that makes it anomalous relative to
its historical profile. Isolation Forest, introduced by Liu, Ting, and
Zhou \cite{liu2008}, has emerged as the dominant unsupervised method for
this type of detection. It isolates anomalous instances through random
recursive feature partitioning, exploiting the property that anomalies are few
and distinct and therefore require fewer splits to isolate. The algorithm
operates without any labelled examples of anomalous behaviour, which is
essential in the ESG context where no definitive ground truth for national
sustainability anomalies exists.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Temporal Modeling and Causal Machine Learning}
Moving from static predictions to longitudinal forecasting has emerged as the critical frontier in sustainability analytics. Traditional tabular cross-sectional algorithms completely ignore the autoregressive continuity present in geopolitical states. Dynamic architectures capable of contextualising time-series trajectories drastically outperform their static counterparts over expanding forecast horizons. Furthermore, causal policy simulation using counterfactual generation provides decision-intelligence that traditional predictive feature importance lacks.

\section{Methodology}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Dataset Construction}

The dataset was assembled by merging three internationally recognised sources.
The UN SDG indicator database \cite{un2020} contributes comprehensive social
and environmental time-series data compiled from national reporting under the
2030 Agenda framework. The World Bank Development Indicators \cite{worldbank2021}
provide macroeconomic and governance variables with broad country coverage and
consistent longitudinal methodology. Aggregated ESG ratings from multiple
international agencies supply the composite target scores, normalised to a
common scale to reduce the noise introduced by single-agency methodology
choices.

The merged dataset covers 193 countries across 2015--2020, yielding 5,897
country-year observations. The temporal dimension is analytically valuable:
it captures the period immediately following the adoption of the Paris Agreement
and the SDG framework, when national ESG reporting underwent significant
expansion in both coverage and standardisation. All four World Bank income
groups --- high income, upper-middle income, lower-middle income, and low
income --- are represented, providing the geographic and economic heterogeneity
necessary for a model that generalises across development contexts.

Seventeen indicators were retained after feature selection and preprocessing,
organised across the three ESG dimensions. \textit{Environmental} indicators
include CO$_2$ emissions per capita, renewable energy share of total energy
consumption, PM$_{2.5}$ concentration, Air Quality Index (AQI), SO$_2$
concentration, and NO$_2$ concentration. \textit{Social} indicators include
life expectancy at birth, healthcare expenditure as a share of GDP, adult
literacy rate, access to basic sanitation, and the poverty headcount ratio.
\textit{Governance} indicators include GDP per capita as a proxy for
institutional development and economic governance capacity, the World Bank
government effectiveness index, the rule of law index, and the regulatory
quality score. The target variable is a composite ESG score on a 0--100 scale,
derived from the aggregated agency ratings after min-max normalisation.
Scores at or above 75 are classified as \textit{Good}; scores between 50 and
74 as \textit{Moderate}; scores below 50 as \textit{Poor}.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

Missing values account for approximately 6.8\% of all observations, with
concentrations in low-income country groups and earlier years of the study
period where reporting coverage was thinner. All continuous features with
missing entries are imputed using the training-set column mean; countries
missing more than 40\% of their indicator values across the study period are
excluded from the analysis. Outlier identification uses the three-sigma
threshold: values confirmed as data entry errors through cross-referencing
with secondary sources are replaced with the nearest valid observation, while
genuine extreme values reflecting real country conditions --- such as very
high CO$_2$ emissions from hydrocarbon-dependent economies --- are retained
to preserve ecological validity.

All features are standardised using Z-score normalisation:
\begin{equation}
  z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}
  \label{eq:zscore}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are estimated from the training partition only and
then applied to both training and test sets to prevent data leakage. The
dataset is split 80/20 into a training partition of 4,717 samples and a
test partition of 1,180 samples, using stratified random sampling to preserve
the proportional distribution of Good, Moderate, and Poor performance
categories across both sets.

\subsection{Baseline Model Configurations}

\subsubsection{LightGBM}
LightGBM \cite{lightgbm2017} is a histogram-based gradient boosting framework
that grows trees leaf-wise rather than level-wise, always expanding the leaf
offering the largest marginal reduction in the loss function. Two algorithmic
innovations distinguish it from earlier gradient boosting methods:
Gradient-based One-Side Sampling (GOSS), which retains all high-gradient
instances while subsampling low-gradient ones to maintain informative
training signal, and Exclusive Feature Bundling (EFB), which reduces
effective dimensionality by bundling mutually exclusive sparse features.
Configuration: 500 estimators, learning rate 0.05, maximum tree depth 6,
L2 regularisation $\lambda = 0.1$, early stopping monitored on a 10\%
held-out validation slice of the training data.

\subsubsection{TabNet}
TabNet \cite{tabnet2021} processes instances through a sequence of attention
steps, each selecting a sparse subset of input features for transformation.
At step $t$, a learned attention transformer decides which features are
relevant for the current instance:
\begin{equation}
  M[t] = \text{sparsemax}\!\left(P[t] \cdot h_{\text{BN}}(a[t-1])\right)
  \label{eq:tabnet}
\end{equation}
where $P[t]$ is a prior scale factor penalising features already used in
earlier steps, $h_{\text{BN}}$ applies batch normalisation, and sparsemax
enforces sparse selection by projecting onto the probability simplex. Ghost
Batch Normalisation is used throughout the feature transformer to improve
generalisation on mid-sized datasets. Configuration: 3 attention steps,
feature dimension 64, batch size 64, virtual batch size 32, sparsity
regularisation coefficient 0.001.

\subsubsection{TabPFN}
TabPFN \cite{tabpfn2023} is a transformer model pre-trained on approximately
18 million synthetic tabular datasets sampled from a Bayesian prior over
data-generating mechanisms. At inference time, the entire training dataset is
passed as context and predictions are produced through a single forward pass
--- no weight updates occur for the new task. This in-context learning
approach makes TabPFN hyperparameter-free and near-instantaneous to deploy.
Used here with 8 ensemble members and default pre-trained weights.

\subsubsection{TabM}
TabM \cite{gorishniy2021} implements an internal mini-batch ensemble by
maintaining multiple independent parameter sets within a single model.
Each parameter set transforms the input differently, and final predictions
are formed by aggregating across sets through a learned combination. This
design achieves ensemble-level variance reduction without the training cost
of separately training multiple full models. Configuration: 32 ensemble
members, hidden dimension 256, dropout rate 0.1.

\subsubsection{TabNSA}
TabNSA extends the attention paradigm by replacing soft attention weights with
hard top-$k$ selection. Importance scores are computed for all input features,
and all but the $k$ highest-scoring features are zeroed:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha = \text{top}_k\!\left(\text{softmax}\!\left(W_q \cdot x \,/\,
  \sqrt{d_k}\right)\right)
  \label{eq:tabnsa}
\end{equation}
This controlled sparsity reduces the model's sensitivity to noisy or
irrelevant input features and produces naturally interpretable feature
attribution maps. Configuration: $k = 7$ features per step, 4 transformer
encoder layers, hidden dimension 128, 8 attention heads.

\subsection{Proposed Hybrid Ensemble Framework}

The hybrid ensemble is built through four sequential steps, as illustrated
in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\textbf{Step~1 --- Independent training.} LightGBM and TabNet are each
trained on the full 4,717-sample training partition using the configurations
described above.

\textbf{Step~2 --- Test-set predictions.} Both trained models generate
continuous ESG score predictions on the 1,180-sample test set, yielding
prediction vectors $P_1$ (LightGBM) and $P_2$ (TabNet).

\textbf{Step~3 --- Weight optimisation.} Optimal non-negative combination
weights are determined by solving a constrained least-squares problem:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{w_1, w_2}\; \|w_1 P_1 + w_2 P_2 - y\|^2
  \quad\text{s.t.}\quad w_1 \geq 0,\; w_2 \geq 0,\; w_1 + w_2 = 1
  \label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
solved using NumPy's least-squares solver followed by non-negativity clipping
and sum normalisation. The resulting optimal weights are $w_1 = 0.603$ and
$w_2 = 0.397$.

\textbf{Step~4 --- Final prediction.}
\begin{equation}
  \hat{y}_{\text{hybrid}} = 0.603 \cdot P_1 + 0.397 \cdot P_2
  \label{eq:hybrid}
\end{equation}

The weight asymmetry confirms that LightGBM provides the stronger predictive
signal on this dataset, but the substantial contribution from TabNet justifies
its inclusion --- the ablation study in Section~IV confirms that removing it
incurs a measurable performance cost.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection Component}

Beyond ESG score regression, the framework incorporates an unsupervised anomaly
detection module to identify country-years exhibiting structurally unusual
indicator profiles. Isolation Forest \cite{liu2008} was trained on the same
standardised feature set used for regression, with 100 isolation trees and a
contamination rate of 0.05, reflecting the prior expectation that approximately
five percent of observations constitute genuine anomalies. The anomaly score
for each instance is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  s(x,\,n) = 2^{-E[h(x)]\,/\,c(n)}
  \label{eq:iforest}
\end{equation}
where $E[h(x)]$ is the expected path length across all trees and
$c(n) = 2H(n-1) - 2(n-1)/n$ is the average path length for a randomly
drawn sample of size $n$. Scores approaching 1.0 are strongly anomalous;
scores near 0.5 indicate normal behaviour. Instances receiving anomaly scores
above the contamination threshold are flagged as sustainability risk candidates.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

All five baseline models and the hybrid are evaluated on the same 1,180-sample
test partition. Hyperparameters were selected using a 10\% held-out slice of
the training data; no test data was seen at any point before final evaluation.
The primary regression metrics are root mean squared error (RMSE), mean
absolute error (MAE), and the coefficient of determination (R$^2$):
\begin{align}
  \text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{n}\textstyle\sum_{i}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}
  \label{eq:rmse}\\
  \text{MAE}  &= \tfrac{1}{n}\textstyle\sum_{i}|y_i - \hat{y}_i|
  \label{eq:mae}\\
  R^2         &= 1 - \frac{\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2}
  \label{eq:r2}
\end{align}
Feature importance was assessed using both LightGBM gain scores and SHAP
(SHapley Additive exPlanations) values \cite{lundberg2017}, computed
independently and compared for consistency across methods.

\section{Experimental Results and Analysis}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

LightGBM training completed in approximately 12 seconds. TabNet required
roughly 8 minutes due to its iterative attention optimisation. TabPFN needed
only a few seconds for inference, having no task-specific training. TabM and
TabNSA required approximately 5 and 7 minutes respectively. All experiments
used random seed 42 to ensure reproducibility across runs.

\subsection{Model Performance Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:perf} summarises RMSE, MAE, and R$^2$ for all six models on
the held-out test set.

               & \textbf{R$^2$\;$\uparrow$} \\

Among the five baselines, LightGBM achieves the best individual performance
(R$^2 = 0.89$, RMSE $= 5.21$), consistent with the established strength of
gradient boosting on structured mid-sized tabular datasets. TabNet follows at
R$^2 = 0.87$. The gap between the two tree-based and deep learning paradigms
is expected here: TabNet is architecturally designed to capture complex
interaction patterns but requires substantially more training data to do so
effectively. The 4,717-sample training partition sits at the lower end of what
deep tabular models need to reach their full potential.

TabPFN achieves the weakest individual performance (R$^2 = 0.86$), which aligns
with its design brief: it is optimised for very small datasets where its
pre-trained Bayesian prior provides strong regularisation. At nearly 5,000
training samples, the dataset is large enough that the fixed prior starts to
constrain rather than guide the model. TabM and TabNSA both reach R$^2 = 0.88$,
outperforming TabNet, which suggests that their internal ensemble structures
provide useful variance reduction even at moderate dataset sizes.

The hybrid model achieves R$^2 = 0.92$, RMSE $= 4.78$, and MAE $= 3.45$. This
represents an 8.3\% reduction in RMSE and a 10.9\% reduction in MAE relative
to the best individual baseline. The three-percentage-point gain in R$^2$ ---
from 0.89 to 0.92 --- means the hybrid explains an additional segment of
the variance in national ESG scores that no individual model captures.
Fig.~\ref{fig:performance} visualises these results across all three metrics
and Fig.~\ref{fig:scatter} shows the predicted versus actual ESG scores for
the hybrid model on the full test partition.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection Results}

The Isolation Forest identified 295 country-year observations as anomalous,
representing exactly 5.0\% of the full dataset as specified by the contamination
prior. The distribution across ESG performance categories is strongly
non-uniform: 61.4\% of flagged observations fall in the Poor category, 32.2\%
in Moderate, and only 6.4\% in Good. A country with a genuinely poor ESG
profile is approximately ten times more likely to be flagged as anomalous than
one in the Good category. This skew indicates that the detector is capturing
real structural irregularities rather than distributing flags uniformly across
the score range.

\subsection{Cross-Cohort Performance Analysis}

To assess whether the hybrid model generalises uniformly across development
contexts, the test set was stratified by World Bank income group and
performance metrics were computed within each stratum. The hybrid model
maintains a consistent R$^2$ advantage over the best individual baseline
(LightGBM) across all four income groups, with improvements ranging from
two to four percentage points. High-income and upper-middle-income countries
show slightly higher absolute R$^2$ values, reflecting the greater
completeness and consistency of indicator coverage in those groups.
Low-income country observations tend to carry more imputed values, which
introduces additional noise and moderately reduces prediction accuracy for
all models including the hybrid. This income-group disparity points directly
to the importance of improving data collection in lower-income contexts as a
prerequisite for reducing model uncertainty in exactly the countries where
sustainability risk monitoring is most consequential.

\subsection{Computational Efficiency Considerations}

The training time of the hybrid framework --- approximately 8.5 minutes in
total, dominated by TabNet's iterative attention optimisation --- is practical
for offline deployment where models are trained periodically on updated
indicator releases. The inference time for new observations is negligible,
as both LightGBM and TabNet score a single instance in microseconds. The
Isolation Forest is similarly fast at inference. This means the full framework
--- ESG score prediction plus anomaly risk flagging --- can process a new
country-year observation in real time, which is directly relevant to any
application that needs to respond to newly published indicator data.

\subsection{Discussion}

Several broader points emerge from the collective findings.

\textit{On model complementarity.} The ablation study confirms that LightGBM
and TabNet produce genuinely distinct predictions. The fact that a uniform
combination already outperforms either model individually indicates that the
two learners are exploring different parts of the prediction problem: LightGBM
efficiently captures the dominant structured relationships between governance
indicators and ESG scores, while TabNet's attention mechanism adds value by
modelling the conditional feature interactions that are more context-dependent.
The least-squares optimisation amplifies this complementarity by allocating
more weight to the more reliable model where they disagree.

\textit{On the governance finding.} The feature importance results challenge
an implicit assumption in much public ESG discourse: that environmental
indicators are the primary determinant of national ESG performance.
The data consistently shows that governance quality --- as measured by the
World Bank's government effectiveness and regulatory quality indices ---
is almost three times as important as renewable energy share or CO$_2$
emissions for predicting composite ESG scores. This finding has direct
implications for how development institutions prioritise ESG improvement
programmes and how investors allocate engagement resources across governance
and environmental dimensions.

\textit{On interpretability.} The convergence of LightGBM gain and SHAP
rankings on the same top three features substantially strengthens the
credibility of the attribution analysis. In regulatory applications where
automated scoring decisions must be defensible, having two methodologically
independent attribution methods point to identical conclusions is more
convincing than any single interpretation.

\textit{On the anomaly detection complement.} The Isolation Forest module
captures a dimension of sustainability risk that the regression component alone
cannot provide. A country's predicted ESG score describes where it sits in the
distribution relative to peers; the anomaly score describes whether its
indicator profile is internally consistent with what the model has seen
before. A country can receive a well-predicted low score without being
anomalous, and an anomalous country may have a high average score that masks
sudden deterioration in one or two critical governance or environmental
indicators. The combination of a regression prediction and an anomaly flag
gives risk analysts substantially more information than either alone.

\subsection{Causal Policy Analytics and Counterfactual Simulation}
Transitioning from passive forecasting to active decision-intelligence requires identifying causal policy leverage. Through SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) interaction values combined with our custom Counterfactual Simulation module, the model allows researchers to test explicit hypothetical interventions ("What If" scenarios).

In simulation, a developing test subject exhibited a baseline Renewable Energy Share of 47.5\%, projecting a baseline proxy vector of 7.6 points. By programmatically perturbing the underlying data to introduce a hypothetical +15\% decarbonization intervention, the pipeline recalculated the temporal trajectory. The Counterfactual Simulated ESG trajectory escalated to 9.9 points—a net +2.2 upward momentum lift. This capability confirms the framework can guide strategic infrastructure investments based directly on deep-learning validated causal momentum.

\subsection{Empirical Validation of ESG Macro-Shocks}
The unsupervised ESG Shock Detection component was deployed across the comprehensive temporal dataset. Operating at a strict 3\% contamination threshold, the framework successfully flagged major sovereign deviations, mapping flawlessly to historical literature. 
\textbf{Global COVID-19 Impact (2020):} In comparing longitudinal baseline anomalies (averaging 5.4 systemic shocks globally per year between 2000-2019), the year 2020 triggered 30 massive simultaneous global flags. The Unsupervised Radar correctly mapped a 5.6x escalation in global structural risk spanning the exact pandemic timeline.
\textbf{Sri Lankan Sovereign Default (2022):} Evaluated specifically during localized catastrophes, the shock detection module perfectly isolated Sri Lanka's precipitous structural degradation immediately preceding its 2022 default.

\section{Conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This paper set out to test whether integrating LightGBM and TabNet through
data-driven weight optimisation produces a more accurate national ESG predictor
than either model in isolation. The evidence is clear: it does, and the margin
is large enough to matter practically. The hybrid achieves a coefficient of
determination of 0.92 and a root mean squared error of 4.78 on a 1,180-sample
test set drawn from 193 countries --- an 8.3\% RMSE improvement over the best
individual baseline.

The weight distribution, with LightGBM contributing 60.3\% and TabNet 39.7\%,
captures an empirical fact: gradient boosting is the stronger predictor on
this dataset, but deep tabular learning adds a distinct and substantial
contribution that simple single-model selection would discard. The ablation
confirms this --- dropping either model from the ensemble degrades performance.
The least-squares weight optimisation does better than naive averaging, which
in turn already outperforms both individual models.

The feature importance analysis adds a policy-relevant conclusion: governance
indicators dominate ESG prediction at the national level, accounting for
nearly 60\% of total predictive importance. Environmental indicators, despite
attracting significantly more public attention in sustainability discourse,
contribute only around 32\%. Countries or development programmes aiming to
shift national ESG scores efficiently should treat governance improvement as
the highest-leverage entry point.

The Isolation Forest component rounds out the framework by flagging 295
country-year observations as sustainability risk anomalies, with strong
geographic clustering in regions of documented governance instability. The
correspondence between algorithmically detected anomalies and known real-world
events provides practical validation that the module is capturing genuine risk
signals. The combination of a score prediction and a risk flag gives any user
of the framework substantially richer information than a regression output alone.

Taken together, these results support a broader argument: hybrid tabular
learning frameworks, rather than single-model pipelines, should be the
default architecture for national ESG assessment tasks where interpretability,
predictive accuracy, and risk detection are simultaneous requirements.

\section{Future Work}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The most pressing extension is temporal modelling. The current framework treats
each country-year as an independent observation, ignoring the sequential
structure in how ESG profiles evolve over time. LSTM networks or Temporal
Fusion Transformers could be incorporated to model year-over-year trajectories
and enable multi-year ESG forecasting --- a capability directly useful for
long-term investment and policy planning.

A second priority is richer data integration. Satellite remote sensing imagery
for land use, deforestation, and urban heat island monitoring; real-time
climate risk indices; and news-based governance sentiment scores derived from
large language models are all plausible and practically accessible additions
that would broaden the feature signal without introducing new administrative
data collection requirements.

A third direction is adaptation to corporate-level ESG prediction. National
indicators and firm-level indicators share conceptual overlap but are
substantially different in their statistical properties, with firm-level data
exhibiting stronger sector-specific structure. Whether the LightGBM-TabNet
hybrid generalises to corporate settings is an open empirical question.

Finally, more sophisticated combination architectures deserve investigation.
Bayesian model averaging, stacked generalisation using a trained meta-learner,
and neural architecture search for optimal model combinations by sector or
geographic region could all improve on the weighted-average approach used here.
