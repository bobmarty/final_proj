  ESG PREDICTION PROJECT — COMPLETE EXPLANATION
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT PROBLEM IT SOLVES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Most countries get ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) scores from rating
agencies using manual formulas — weighted sums of indicators. These are
descriptive, not predictive. They tell you what happened, not what will happen.

This project builds a machine learning system that:
  1. Predicts a country's future ESG score using last year's data
  2. Detects countries in structural crisis before their score collapses
  3. Explains which factors drive a country's sustainability performance

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE DATA (Real World Bank Data)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Source        : World Bank Open Data API (fully citable)
  Coverage      : 252 countries, 2002–2023, 5,544 observations
  Databases used:
    - World Development Indicators (WDI), World Bank Group
    - Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI), World Bank Group

Three pillars of indicators are collected:

  ENVIRONMENTAL
    - PM2.5 pollution exposure (µg/m³)       — lower is better
    - Renewable energy share (% total energy)
    - Forest area (% of land)
    - CO₂ emissions per capita (metric tons)  — lower is better

  SOCIAL
    - Life expectancy at birth (years)
    - Health expenditure (% of GDP)
    - GDP per capita (constant 2015 USD)
    - Unemployment rate (%)                   — lower is better

  GOVERNANCE  (World Governance Indicators — WGI scale: −2.5 to +2.5)
    - Government effectiveness
    - Rule of law
    - Control of corruption
    - Political stability

Key insight from the paper: Governance indicators are the STRONGEST predictors
(~58.7% of feature importance) — far more than environmental metrics, which
receive most of the public attention.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE ESG SCORE (Target Variable)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The target esg_score is a three-pillar composite built transparently:

  Environmental score = avg(CO₂ inverted, renewable %, forest %, PM2.5 inverted)
  Social score        = avg(life expectancy, health exp, GDP per capita, unemp inverted)
  Governance score    = avg(political stability, rule of law, corruption, govt effectiveness)

  ESG Score = (E_score + S_score + G_score) / 3   →  scaled 0 to 100

All indicators are min-max normalised globally across all country-years before
averaging, so a rich country's GDP does not dominate a poor country's life
expectancy. The final score is mapped to three categories:
  - Good     : Score >= 75
  - Moderate : Score 50 – 74
  - Poor     : Score < 50

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TEMPORAL FEATURE ENGINEERING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Raw data is one number per country per year. The model needs historical context,
so every indicator is expanded into 6 derived features:

  renewable_energy_pct             ← current value
  renewable_energy_pct_lag1        ← 1 year ago (T-1)
  renewable_energy_pct_lag2        ← 2 years ago (T-2)
  renewable_energy_pct_lag3        ← 3 years ago (T-3)
  renewable_energy_pct_roll3_mean  ← 3-year rolling average
  renewable_energy_pct_roll3_std   ← 3-year rolling volatility (shock indicator)

This turns 12 base indicators into 69 features per row, giving the model a view
of both the current state and historical trajectory of every country.

Why this matters: A sudden spike in PM2.5 volatility (roll3_std) signals an
environmental shock even if the current value looks normal. The rolling std
features act as macro-shock detectors.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE 5 MACHINE LEARNING MODELS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The paper benchmarks five different modelling approaches:

  1. LightGBM  (R²=0.89 — best individual model)
     - Gradient boosting decision tree
     - Grows trees leaf-wise — very efficient on tabular data
     - Captures non-linear interactions between governance and environmental features
     - Config: 500 trees, learning rate 0.05, max depth 6, L2 regularisation 0.1

  2. TabNet  (R²=0.87)
     - Deep learning architecture designed specifically for tabular data
     - Uses sequential attention — for each country it selects which features
       matter most at each processing step
     - Different countries activate different feature subsets
       (e.g. oil states rely more on energy features)
     - Config: 3 attention steps, feature dimension 64, batch size 64

  3. TabPFN  (R²=0.86)
     - Pre-trained transformer — zero training required on new data
     - Uses in-context learning: the entire training set becomes the prompt
     - Fastest to run but plateaus on larger datasets (design limitation)
     - Config: 8 ensemble members, default pre-trained weights

  4. TabM  (R²=0.88)
     - Internal mini-ensemble: 32 small neural networks inside one model
     - Each member sees the data differently, then their predictions are combined
     - Gets ensemble variance reduction at the computational cost of one model
     - Config: 32 members, hidden dimension 256, dropout 0.1

  5. TabNSA  (R²=0.88)
     - Hard sparse attention: selects only the top-7 most relevant features
       per prediction, zeroing out all others
     - Forces the model to be selective — reduces noise from irrelevant indicators
     - Config: k=7 features per step, 4 transformer layers, 8 attention heads,
       hidden dimension 128

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE HYBRID MODEL  (R²=0.92 — best result)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The central finding of the paper: combining LightGBM and TabNet beats both
individually by a meaningful margin.

The combination weights are not guessed — they are solved mathematically using
constrained least-squares optimisation:

  Minimise:  ||w1 * P_lgbm + w2 * P_tabnet - y||²
  Subject to: w1 >= 0,  w2 >= 0,  w1 + w2 = 1

Result: LightGBM 60.3%  +  TabNet 39.7%

Why does this work? The two models make different errors:
  - LightGBM is stronger on tree-structured feature relationships
  - TabNet captures attention-based feature interaction patterns
Combining them reduces total prediction variance.

ABLATION PROOF (paper Table 2):
  Configuration                  RMSE     R²
  LightGBM only                  5.21    0.89
  TabNet only                    5.45    0.87
  Hybrid — uniform (0.50/0.50)   5.02    0.91
  Hybrid — optimal (0.603/0.397) 4.78    0.92   ← best

Even a naive 50/50 blend beats both individual models, confirming the two
learners contribute genuinely different and complementary signals.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ANOMALY DETECTION — ISOLATION FOREST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Separate from prediction, the system runs an unsupervised anomaly detector that
finds countries with structurally unusual ESG indicator patterns.

How Isolation Forest works:
  - Builds 100 random trees by recursively splitting the feature space
  - Anomalous countries are isolated in very few splits — they are structurally
    different from normal countries
  - Countries requiring many splits to isolate are considered normal
  - Contamination = 5% → flags 278 out of 5,544 observations as anomalous

What it catches:
  - Countries with high GDP but collapsing governance (resource-curse states)
  - Environmental shocks: sudden PM2.5 spikes despite stable social indicators
  - Pre-crisis signals: deteriorating rule of law before a sovereign default
  - Pandemic-year structural breaks (e.g. 2020 global disruption)

This component answers a different question from prediction:
  - Prediction asks:  "What score should this country receive?"
  - Anomaly asks:     "Does this country's indicator pattern look structurally unusual?"
A country can have a well-predicted low score without being anomalous, and an
anomalous country may have a high average score that masks sudden deterioration.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
CHRONOLOGICAL TRAIN/TEST SPLIT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The model is trained on data up to 2018, then tested on 2019–2023 data it has
never seen:

  Training set : countries × years 2002–2018  (4,284 rows, 77%)
  Test set     : countries × years 2019–2023  (1,260 rows, 23%)

This is called out-of-time (OOT) validation — it simulates real forecasting.
Random splits cannot be used for time-series data because future data would leak
into training (the model would effectively "know" the future), artificially
inflating all metrics.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FEATURE IMPORTANCE — SHAP ANALYSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

After training, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values explain why the
model made each prediction by computing each feature's average contribution:

  Rank  Feature                   LightGBM Gain   Mean |SHAP|
  1     Government Effectiveness    0.248           8.92
  2     GDP per Capita              0.187           7.15
  3     Regulatory Quality          0.152           5.83
  4     Renewable Energy Share      0.134           4.97
  5     CO₂ Emissions per Capita    0.121           4.61
  6     Life Expectancy             0.089           3.22
  7     PM2.5 Concentration         0.069           2.45

Governance features (ranks 1, 2, 3) account for ~58.7% of total predictive power.
Environmental features (ranks 4, 5, 7) account for ~32.4%.
Social features (rank 6) account for ~8.9%.

Both LightGBM gain and SHAP values rank features in the same order — this
consistency across two independent methods strengthens the finding's credibility.

Policy implication: Countries looking to improve their ESG score most efficiently
should focus on governance reforms first, not environmental spending.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
IN ONE SENTENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The project takes 12 real World Bank indicators for every country, engineers 69
temporal features from them, trains 5 different ML models on historical data up
to 2018, combines the best two into a hybrid via constrained least-squares
(R²=0.92), and uses an Isolation Forest to flag countries in structural ESG
crisis — all to prove that governance quality, not environmental metrics, is the
dominant driver of national sustainability scores.
