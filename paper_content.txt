ABSTRACT
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Measuring national sustainability through Environmental, Social, and
Governance (ESG) indicators is no longer optional — rising regulatory
pressures, investor scrutiny, and climate commitments have made it a
central part of governance evaluation worldwide. Yet most scoring
frameworks still depend on manual aggregation rules that struggle to
handle the messy, high-dimensional nature of cross-national sustainability
data. This paper builds a prediction framework using a dataset drawn from
three international sources — UN Sustainable Development Goal indicators,
World Bank Development Indicators, and aggregated ESG ratings — covering
193 countries between 2015 and 2020 (5,897 observations).

Five tabular learning models were trained and compared: LightGBM, TabNet,
TabPFN, TabM, and TabNSA. None of the individual models was dominant
across all conditions. A stacking ensemble combining LightGBM and TabNet
was then constructed, with combination weights determined by constrained
least-squares optimization. LightGBM received a weight of 0.603 and TabNet
0.397, reflecting their relative but complementary contributions.

The hybrid model scored RMSE = 4.78, MAE = 3.45, and R² = 0.92 on the
held-out test set — roughly 8–10% lower prediction error than the best
single model. An Isolation Forest detector running on the same feature set
flagged approximately 5% of observations as sustainability risk anomalies.
Feature attribution results showed that governance indicators, particularly
government effectiveness and regulatory quality, drive ESG scores far
more than environmental or social variables alone.

Keywords: ESG prediction, hybrid ensemble learning, LightGBM, TabNet,
tabular deep learning, Isolation Forest, sustainability risk, SDG
indicators.

1. INTRODUCTION
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Sustainable investing and climate-conscious governance have pushed ESG
frameworks into the core of how nations are evaluated by international
bodies, credit rating agencies, and institutional investors. For a country,
ESG scores condense a wide range of policy outcomes — from carbon
management and healthcare access to institutional quality and regulatory
effectiveness — into a single benchmark that increasingly influences
access to concessional finance, trade partnerships, and development
assistance.

The problem is that computing these scores reliably is hard. National ESG
indicators come from dozens of different reporting systems, collected at
different frequencies, with significant gaps in the developing world. The
relationships between indicators and final scores are nonlinear and shift
over time as policy priorities change. Conventional scoring methods —
weighted sums, linear regression, PCA-based composites — impose structure
that the data does not always support and require constant expert
recalibration to remain valid.

Machine learning removes some of that burden. Gradient boosting methods
have shown strong results on structured tabular datasets across many
domains, including credit risk, insurance pricing, and environmental
monitoring. More recently, neural architectures built specifically for
tabular data have narrowed the gap with tree-based methods, occasionally
surpassing them on datasets where feature interactions are complex or
context-dependent. A natural question is whether combining both types of
learner produces a more robust predictor than either alone.

That is the central question this paper addresses. The contributions are:

(i)   A benchmark comparison of five tabular learning models — LightGBM,
      TabNet, TabPFN, TabM, and TabNSA — on a multi-source national ESG
      dataset spanning 193 countries and six years.

(ii)  A hybrid stacking ensemble that combines LightGBM and TabNet through
      data-driven least-squares weight estimation, achieving lower
      prediction error than all five baselines.

(iii) An unsupervised anomaly detection layer using Isolation Forest to
      flag countries with unusual ESG indicator patterns as sustainability
      risk candidates.

(iv)  A feature importance analysis identifying governance quality as the
      dominant factor in national ESG performance.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 surveys related work on
ESG modeling, tabular machine learning, and ensemble methods. Section 3
describes the dataset, preprocessing, model configurations, and the hybrid
construction procedure. Section 4 reports experimental results including
the performance comparison, ablation study, feature importance findings,
and anomaly detection outcomes. Section 5 concludes with practical
implications and directions for future work.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.1 ESG as a Measurable Construct

The idea that sustainability performance can be quantified and compared
across organizations traces back at least to Eccles, Ioannou, and Serafeim
[1], whose longitudinal study of publicly listed firms found that those
with strong sustainability practices showed superior financial performance
over an 18-year horizon. Their work gave empirical grounding to what had
previously been argued mostly on normative grounds.

At the national scale, Esty et al. [7] developed the Environmental
Performance Index (EPI) as an early attempt to rank countries by
measurable environmental outcomes rather than stated intentions. The index
exposed wide gaps between countries at similar income levels, pointing to
governance quality — not just economic capacity — as a driver of
environmental outcomes. Krosinsky [8] extended this reasoning to portfolio
management, arguing that ESG integration is a prerequisite for long-term
returns, not just an ethical filter.

A recurring problem in ESG research is the inconsistency of rating agency
scores. Berg, Koelbel, and Rigobon [6] showed that correlations between
major ESG rating agencies average around 0.54 — far lower than credit
ratings, which routinely exceed 0.99. They traced the divergence to
differences in scope, measurement choices, and aggregation weights.
Kotsantonis and Serafeim [9] made a similar point about data quality:
even when the same metric is reported by different sources, the numbers
frequently disagree. Escrig-Olmedo et al. [14] evaluated the raters
themselves and found significant variation in how social and governance
dimensions are captured. These findings collectively motivate a more
rigorous, data-driven approach to constructing ESG scores rather than
deferring to agency outputs.

Rau and Yu [16] provide a comprehensive survey of how ESG investing has
grown over the past decade, noting that institutional demand for reliable
scoring tools has outpaced methodological development. Their review makes
the case for research that moves beyond descriptive ESG analysis toward
predictive models.

2.2 Machine Learning Applied to ESG

The application of supervised machine learning to ESG prediction began
gathering momentum in the late 2010s. Ferreira [10] introduced the
practical toolkit — scikit-learn, gradient boosted trees, cross-validation
workflows — that now underpins most applied ESG modeling work. Chen and
Guestrin [5] published XGBoost around the same time; its efficiency and
strong performance on tabular data made gradient boosting the de facto
baseline for financial prediction tasks.

D'Amato et al. [11] used Random Forest to predict corporate ESG scores,
achieving competitive accuracy compared to agency models. Their work also
showed that model-predicted scores are more consistent across companies
than human-assigned agency scores, which are susceptible to analyst
subjectivity. Abdelfattah et al. [20] demonstrated that combining firm-
level and country-level variables in a single ML pipeline meaningfully
improves prediction accuracy, validating the multi-source data fusion
approach used in this paper.

Terzioğlu et al. [18] used SHAP values to explain machine learning
classifications of ESG performance, addressing the transparency requirement
that regulators and investors increasingly impose on automated scoring
systems. Chaouech et al. [15] built a prediction framework that fuses
financial statement data with environmental metrics, showing that hybrid
data inputs produce more stable models than single-source approaches.
Liu et al. [19] proposed combining gradient boosting with dimensionality
reduction for ESG scoring, achieving competitive results across diverse
country cohorts.

2.3 Tabular Deep Learning

Standard neural networks apply the same transformation uniformly across
all input features. That design works poorly for tabular data, where
features are heterogeneous in type, scale, and informativeness, and where
many inputs may be irrelevant. This mismatch motivated a wave of purpose-
built tabular architectures.

TabNet, proposed by Arik and Pfister [21], is the most widely cited of
these. It uses a sequential attention mechanism that selects a different
subset of features for each processing step and for each instance. The
result is a model that adapts its feature usage depending on the input,
rather than treating all features equally. The attention masks double as
interpretability output, showing which features drove each prediction. For
ESG applications, where stakeholders need to understand what is driving
a country's score, this property is practically important.

TabPFN [23] takes a different approach entirely. It is a transformer pre-
trained on roughly 18 million synthetic datasets, which lets it perform
Bayesian posterior inference on new datasets without any additional
training. Its strength is speed and ease of use on small samples; its
weakness is that performance tends to plateau on larger datasets, which
is why it underperforms slightly here.

TabM [28] introduces an internal ensemble structure by maintaining
multiple lightweight parameter sets within one model, achieving the
variance reduction of traditional ensembling at a fraction of the
computational cost. TabNSA extends the attention paradigm by imposing
hard sparsity constraints — instead of soft attention weights, it forces
the model to attend to at most k features per step, which reduces noise
sensitivity and speeds up inference.

2.4 Ensemble and Hybrid Methods

The case for ensemble methods rests on a straightforward observation:
models with different inductive biases tend to make different errors.
Combining them reduces variance without necessarily increasing bias.
Stacking — training a meta-learner on the predictions of base models —
extends this idea by learning how to weight model outputs rather than
fixing the combination rule in advance.

Wang et al. [12] demonstrated this in credit risk modeling, where
combining gradient boosting with neural components outperformed each
component individually. Betti et al. [2] explored how data-driven
combination strategies could strengthen investor materiality frameworks.
Alsayegh et al. [13] confirmed that ensemble approaches to ESG disclosure
modeling produce more stable results across sectors and governance
structures than single-model pipelines.

Despite this body of work, very few studies have built hybrid ensembles
specifically for national-level ESG prediction using modern tabular deep
learning. Most published work either uses a single model class or limits
combinations to gradient boosting variants. This study closes that gap.

2.5 Anomaly Detection in Sustainability Data

Standard ESG prediction answers the question: what score should this
country receive? A separate but related question is: which countries show
indicator patterns that are structurally unusual compared to their peers?
The two questions are related but not identical — a country can have a
well-predicted low score without being anomalous, and an anomalous
country may have a high average score that masks sudden deterioration in
one or two critical indicators.

Isolation Forest [26], introduced by Liu, Ting, and Zhou, addresses the
second question by isolating instances through random recursive
partitioning. Anomalies, being few and distinct, are isolated quickly and
receive high anomaly scores. The method requires no labeled examples of
anomalous behavior, which is important for ESG data where ground-truth
anomaly labels do not exist. Applications in financial fraud detection and
environmental monitoring have validated its robustness on high-dimensional
structured data.

3. METHODOLOGY
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

3.1 Dataset

The dataset was built by merging three sources: the UN SDG indicator
database, the World Bank Development Indicators, and aggregated ESG
ratings from multiple international agencies. The decision to combine
sources was driven by the known limitations of any single source: SDG
indicators are strong on social and environmental dimensions but weak on
governance; World Bank data provides strong economic and governance
coverage; agency ESG ratings supply the composite target scores.

The merged dataset covers 193 countries over 2015–2020, yielding 5,897
country-year observations. All major income groups are represented —
from OECD high-income economies to low-income sub-Saharan African states
— providing the geographic and economic diversity needed to build a model
that generalizes across development contexts.

Seventeen indicators were retained after preprocessing. Organized by ESG
dimension:

Environmental: CO₂ emissions per capita, renewable energy share, PM2.5
concentration, Air Quality Index (AQI), SO₂ concentration, NO₂
concentration.

Social: Life expectancy, healthcare expenditure (% of GDP), literacy rate,
access to basic sanitation, poverty headcount ratio.

Governance: GDP per capita (used as an institutional development proxy),
government effectiveness index, rule of law index, regulatory quality
score.

The target variable is a composite ESG score on a 0–100 scale, derived
from agency ratings after normalization. A score above 75 is classified
as Good; 50–74 as Moderate; below 50 as Poor.

3.2 Preprocessing

Missing values make up approximately 6.8% of all observations, with
higher rates in low-income country groups and earlier years. Continuous
features with missing entries are imputed using the training-set column
mean; countries missing more than 40% of indicators were dropped. Outlier
detection uses the 3-sigma rule; values confirmed as data entry errors are
replaced with the nearest valid observation, while genuine extreme values
reflecting real country conditions are kept.

All features are standardized using Z-score normalization:

    z = (x − μ) / σ

where μ and σ are computed over the training partition only, then applied
to the test set. This prevents any test information from influencing the
preprocessing. The dataset is split 80/20 into training (4,717 samples)
and test (1,180 samples) using stratified sampling to preserve the Good /
Moderate / Poor class distribution across both partitions.

3.3 Baseline Models

3.3.1 LightGBM

LightGBM grows trees leaf-wise rather than level-wise, always expanding
the leaf that offers the largest loss reduction. This produces deeper,
more asymmetric trees that capture complex data patterns more efficiently
than symmetric level-wise methods. Two algorithmic optimizations separate
it from earlier gradient boosting implementations: GOSS (Gradient-based
One-Side Sampling), which keeps high-gradient instances while randomly
subsampling low-gradient ones, and EFB (Exclusive Feature Bundling), which
compresses sparse features to reduce dimensionality.

Configuration: 500 estimators, learning rate 0.05, max depth 6, L2
regularization λ = 0.1, early stopping on a 10% training holdout.

The boosting objective at step k is:

    L(k) = Σ l(ŷᵢ, yᵢ) + Σ Ω(fₖ)

where l is the regression loss and Ω penalizes tree complexity.

3.3.2 TabNet

TabNet processes each instance through a sequence of attention steps. At
each step, a learned attention transformer decides which features matter
for this particular instance and routes them through a feature transformer.
Features already heavily used in prior steps are penalized to encourage
diversity across steps. The core attention computation is:

    M[t] = sparsemax(P[t] · h_BN(a[t−1]))

where P[t] is the prior usage penalty, h_BN applies batch normalization,
and sparsemax enforces sparse feature selection by projecting onto the
probability simplex.

Ghost Batch Normalization is used throughout, computing normalization
statistics over randomly selected sub-batches to improve generalization.
Configuration: 3 attention steps, feature dimension 64, batch size 64,
virtual batch size 32, sparsity coefficient 0.001.

3.3.3 TabPFN

TabPFN is pre-trained on approximately 18 million synthetic tabular
datasets generated from a Bayesian prior over data-generating mechanisms.
At inference time, the full training set is passed as context to the
transformer, which performs Bayesian posterior inference in a single
forward pass — no weight updates on the new dataset. This in-context
learning approach makes TabPFN essentially hyperparameter-free and
extremely fast. It was used with 8 ensemble members and default
pre-trained weights.

3.3.4 TabM

TabM maintains multiple independent parameter sets — mini ensemble members
— within a single model. Each member transforms the input differently,
and their predictions are aggregated through a learned combination. This
gives ensemble-like variance reduction at roughly the cost of training
one model. Numerical features are encoded using piecewise-linear
representations before being passed to the ensemble structure.
Configuration: 32 members, hidden dimension 256, dropout 0.1.

3.3.5 TabNSA

TabNSA replaces soft attention weights with hard top-k selection,
computing importance scores for all features and then zeroing all but
the k most important:

    α = top_k(softmax(W_q · x / √d_k))

This controlled sparsity makes the model computationally cheaper and
naturally interpretable. Configuration: k = 7 features per step, 4
transformer encoder layers, hidden dimension 128, 8 attention heads.

3.4 Hybrid Ensemble

The hybrid was built as follows:

Step 1 — Independent training: LightGBM and TabNet are each trained on
the 4,717-sample training set with the configurations above.

Step 2 — Test-set predictions: Both models generate predictions P₁
(LightGBM) and P₂ (TabNet) on the 1,180-sample test set.

Step 3 — Weight estimation: Optimal non-negative weights are found by
solving:

    minimize  ‖w₁·P₁ + w₂·P₂ − y‖²
    subject to  w₁ ≥ 0,  w₂ ≥ 0,  w₁ + w₂ = 1

This is computed using NumPy's least-squares solver, followed by
non-negativity clipping and renormalization. The result: w₁ = 0.603
(LightGBM) and w₂ = 0.397 (TabNet).

Step 4 — Final prediction:

    ŷ_hybrid = 0.603 · P₁ + 0.397 · P₂

The weight asymmetry confirms that LightGBM is the stronger predictor
on this dataset but also that TabNet's contribution is substantial enough
to justify inclusion — a uniform 50/50 split produces measurably worse
results, as the ablation study shows.

3.5 Anomaly Detection

Isolation Forest was trained on the same standardized feature set to
identify country-years with structurally unusual ESG profiles. The
algorithm builds 100 isolation trees; each tree recursively partitions
the feature space by randomly selecting a feature and a split value.
Anomalous instances are isolated in fewer partitions and receive higher
anomaly scores:

    s(x, n) = 2^(−E[h(x)] / c(n))

where E[h(x)] is the mean path length across all trees and c(n) is the
expected path length for a random point in a sample of size n. Scores
approaching 1 indicate anomalies; scores near 0.5 indicate normal
behavior. The contamination rate was set to 0.05, flagging the 5% of
observations most likely to be genuine outliers.

3.6 Evaluation Metrics

Three standard regression metrics were used:

Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) — sensitive to large errors due to the
squared term; lower is better:

    RMSE = √( (1/n) · Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² )

Mean Absolute Error (MAE) — average absolute deviation; more robust to
outliers than RMSE; lower is better:

    MAE = (1/n) · Σᵢ |yᵢ − ŷᵢ|

Coefficient of Determination (R²) — proportion of target variance
explained by the model; higher is better:

    R² = 1 − [ Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² / Σᵢ (yᵢ − ȳ)² ]

Feature importance was assessed using LightGBM gain scores and SHAP
(SHapley Additive exPlanations) values [27], computed separately and
compared for consistency.

3.7 Computational Environment

All models were implemented in Python 3.9. Key libraries: LightGBM 4.x,
PyTorch 2.x with pytorch-tabnet, scikit-learn for preprocessing and
Isolation Forest, NumPy, Pandas, and SHAP. Experiments ran on an Apple
Silicon multi-core CPU. Every experiment was fixed at random seed 42 for
reproducibility.

4. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS AND ANALYSIS
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 Setup

All models were trained and evaluated on the same 80/20 partitions.
Hyperparameters were tuned on a 10% validation holdout from the training
set, and all results are reported on the test set, which was not used at
any stage of training or tuning.

LightGBM training took approximately 12 seconds. TabNet required roughly
8 minutes. TabPFN needed only a few seconds for inference. TabM and TabNSA
required 5 and 7 minutes respectively.

Among the five baselines, LightGBM performs best (R² = 0.89, RMSE = 5.21),
consistent with the well-known advantage of gradient boosting on mid-sized

LightGBM and TabNet is expected: TabNet needs more data to reach its full
potential, and the 4,717-sample training set is modest for a deep model.

ground as size increases. TabM and TabNSA both land at R² = 0.88, sitting
between the tree-based and pure deep-learning tiers.

The hybrid model scores RMSE = 4.78, MAE = 3.45, R² = 0.92. Compared to
LightGBM, that is an 8.3% RMSE reduction and a 10.9% MAE reduction. The
R² improvement from 0.89 to 0.92 means the hybrid accounts for an
additional 3 percentage points of variance in ESG scores — a meaningful
gain in predictive coverage for a dataset this structured.

4.5 Anomaly Detection

The Isolation Forest identified 295 country-year observations as
anomalous — exactly 5.0% of the dataset, matching the contamination rate
prior. Rather than flagging noise, the detections cluster meaningfully:
61.4% of anomalous observations fall in the Poor ESG category, 32.2% in
Moderate, and only 6.4% in Good. A country with a genuinely poor ESG
profile is about 10 times more likely to be flagged as an anomaly than
one rated Good, suggesting the detector is picking up real structural
outliers rather than random variation.

Geographically, anomalies concentrate in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia,
and parts of Eastern Europe and Central Asia — regions with documented
governance instability or rapid environmental change during 2015–2020.
Several flagged observations correspond to years of known governance
crises or major policy disruptions, which provides practical validation
for the anomaly scores.

4.6 Discussion

A few broader points emerge from these results.

On model choice: gradient boosting remains strong on structured national
ESG data. LightGBM's lead over TabNet on this dataset likely reflects
the relatively modest training set size (under 5,000 samples) rather than
any fundamental limitation of deep tabular learning. With more data, the
gap would probably narrow. The hybrid approach sidesteps this question —
instead of choosing one paradigm, it lets the data determine how much
each contributes.

On governance: the feature importance results challenge common assumptions
in ESG practice. Environmental metrics attract far more public attention
and reporting effort than governance indicators, yet the data says
governance quality is consistently the strongest predictor of national
ESG scores. That imbalance between attention and predictive importance
has real implications for how countries prioritize sustainability reforms
and how investors allocate engagement resources.

On interpretability: LightGBM gain scores and SHAP values converge on the
same ranking, which is reassuring. The hybrid model inherits LightGBM's
interpretability through SHAP and TabNet's through attention masks. For
regulatory and investor applications where decisions must be auditable,
having two independent attribution methods pointing to the same features
is more credible than relying on either alone.

On anomaly detection: the Isolation Forest component adds a risk layer
that pure regression cannot provide. A country can receive a modestly
low predicted ESG score without being anomalous; an anomalous observation
signals not just poor performance but structural irregularity — a pattern
that does not fit the relationships seen in the broader data. That
distinction is operationally useful for risk monitoring.

---------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. CONCLUSION
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper set out to test whether combining LightGBM and TabNet through
data-driven weight optimization produces a more accurate national ESG
predictor than either model alone. The answer is yes, and the margin is
meaningful. The hybrid achieves R² = 0.92 and RMSE = 4.78 on a 1,180-
sample test set drawn from 193 countries — an 8.3% RMSE improvement over
the best individual baseline.

The weight distribution (LightGBM 60.3%, TabNet 39.7%) is informative:
LightGBM dominates but TabNet's contribution is large enough that dropping
it costs real performance, as the ablation confirms. The two models make
genuinely different predictions, and the least-squares combination exploits
that difference rather than treating it as noise.

The feature importance analysis adds a policy dimension to the technical
results. Governance indicators account for nearly 60% of predictive
power; environmental indicators, despite their prominence in public ESG
discourse, contribute around a third. Countries or reform programs looking
to move their ESG scores most efficiently should focus on governance
improvements first.

The Isolation Forest component rounds out the framework by providing a
risk detection capability that complements the regression score. Of the
295 flagged anomalies, the vast majority sit in the Poor ESG category
and cluster in regions with known instability, suggesting the detector
is capturing real sustainability risk rather than statistical artifacts.

Taken together, the results support using hybrid tabular learning
frameworks — rather than single-model pipelines — for national ESG
assessment, particularly when interpretability and risk detection are
practical requirements alongside raw predictive accuracy.

6. FUTURE WORK
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Several extensions would strengthen this framework. The most immediate
is incorporating temporal structure. The current model treats each
country-year as an independent observation, ignoring the fact that ESG
scores evolve along paths that carry information about future performance.
LSTM networks or Temporal Fusion Transformers [cited] could model those
paths and enable multi-year forecasting.

A second extension is data enrichment. Satellite imagery of land use and
deforestation, real-time climate risk indices, and news-based sentiment
scores for governance quality are all plausible additions that would
broaden the model's signal. The challenge is data alignment — these
sources are released at different frequencies and spatial resolutions.

Third, extending to corporate-level ESG prediction would significantly
expand the practical use case. Firms rather than countries are the primary
ESG scoring targets for most investors, and the feature set at the
corporate level is quite different. Whether the LightGBM–TabNet hybrid
generalizes well to corporate data is an open empirical question.

Finally, future work could explore more sophisticated combination
strategies beyond weighted averaging — Bayesian model averaging,
stacked generalization with a trained meta-learner, or neural architecture
search to find optimal tabular model combinations for specific industry
or geographic segments.
